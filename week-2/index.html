The visual direction

We're not rebranding.
We're remembering.

A classic doesn't chase what's trending. It returns to what's timeless. After four decades, Julie's isn't trying to become a classic. It already is one. So we keep the red and yellow customers love today, and we add back the heritage blue from the original logo.

Keep the Red+Keep the Yellow+Add the Blue

Old and new together: the brand's full original spectrum, refined for today.

The blue was always yours.

This is the original 1981 mark. Red script, blue "Bakeshop," red-blue-yellow stripes. The heritage we're proposing isn't a new idea. It's the brand's own first identity.

The evolution of the mark

Then, little by little, the blue faded out.

From 1981 to today the logo simplified, and the blue slipped away. Our proposal: bring it back on purpose, with modern craft.

The narrative

The point isn't "retro is trendy." It's that the world's biggest brands — Burger King, Adidas, Gap — discovered that their most valuable design asset was the one they abandoned. Julie's doesn't need to invent a new identity. It needs to reclaim the one 40 years of Filipino mornings already built. The old colors aren't a step backward. They're pre-loaded brand equity that a new logo would take decades and millions in media spend to replicate. And the execution principle from the best case studies: treat the past as raw material, not a template to copy — refine proportions, redraw type, build a cohesive system that works across physical and digital.
